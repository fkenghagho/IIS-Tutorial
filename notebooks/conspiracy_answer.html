The Problem with Conspiracy Logic

The exercise highlights a key characteristic of unscientific reasoning: self-sealing logic, where any outcome—affirmation ($E$) or denial ($\lnot E$)—is taken as evidence of the hypothesis ($H$). Bayesian probability theory provides a rigorous framework showing why this is mathematically impossible.

1. Defining Confirmation (Evidence in Favor)

In Bayesian inference, an observation is evidence in favor of a hypothesis ($H$) if the posterior probability $P(H|\text{Observation})$ is strictly greater than the prior probability $P(H)$.

Condition 1: $E$ is evidence in favor of $H$ if:

$$\\P(H|E) > P(H)$$

Condition 2: $\lnot E$ is evidence in favor of $H$ if:

$$\\P(H|\lnot E) > P(H)$$

We assume both Condition 1 and Condition 2 are true, and then show this leads to a contradiction.

2. The Law of Total Probability

The prior probability of $H$ must be the weighted sum of its conditional probabilities, weighted by the probability of the condition itself. This fundamental law is always true for complementary events $E$ and $\lnot E$.

$$\\P(H) = P(H|E) P(E) + P(H|\lnot E) P(\lnot E)$$

3. Proof by Contradiction

Based on our assumption (both conditions are true), we define $\delta_1$ and $\delta_2$ as the positive increases in probability:

$$\\P(H|E) = P(H) + \delta_1 \quad (\delta_1 > 0)$$ $$\\P(H|\lnot E) = P(H) + \delta_2 \quad (\delta_2 > 0)$$

Now, substitute these expressions back into the Law of Total Probability:

$$P(H) = [P(H) + \delta_1] P(E) + [P(H) + \delta_2] P(\lnot E)$$ $$P(H) = P(H) P(E) + \delta_1 P(E) + P(H) P(\lnot E) + \delta_2 P(\lnot E)$$ $$P(H) = P(H) [P(E) + P(\lnot E)] + \delta_1 P(E) + \delta_2 P(\lnot E)$$

Since $P(E) + P(\lnot E) = 1$, we simplify the equation:

$$P(H) = P(H) (1) + \delta_1 P(E) + \delta_2 P(\lnot E)$$ $$P(H) = P(H) + \delta_1 P(E) + \delta_2 P(\lnot E)$$

Subtracting $P(H)$ from both sides yields the final contradiction:

$$\\0 = \delta_1 P(E) + \delta_2 P(\lnot E)$$

4. Conclusion

Since all probabilities ($P(E), P(\lnot E)$) must be positive, and we assumed $\delta_1$ and $\delta_2$ are strictly positive (evidence in favor), their sum must also be strictly positive:

$$\delta_1 P(E) + \delta_2 P(\lnot E) > 0$$

Contradiction: A positive quantity cannot equal zero.

Therefore, the initial assumption that both $E$ and $\lnot E$ can simultaneously increase the probability of $H$ is mathematically impossible within the framework of Bayesian probability theory. If one outcome raises your belief in the hypothesis, the other *must* lower or maintain it.
